"Dark Matter"

Michael Peter Edson

9/9/22

Image shows strong gravitational lensing due to dark matter.

Edson's article "Dark Matter" explores how the vast interworkings of the internet are often forgotten and too often underutilized. Much like how 90% of the interworkings of the universe are made up of invisible dark matter, our modern internet infrastructure and content platforms are built by too often invisible people.

As a Computer Science student, I have great admiration for all the unthanked and often unnamed contributors to FOSS (free open source software) and projects designed to help others. Many people don't realize that it's entirely possible to use the internet without spending any money. This is thanks to people such as Linus Torvalds, the inventor of Linux, a FOSS OS competitor to MAC and Windows. People like Linus have designed FOSS competitors for nearly every major software out there. For example, GIMP is a free alternative to Photoshop, Libre software is a suite of apps that compete with Microsoft Office, and Mozilla Firefox is a FOSS web browser. As a computer science student, the majority of programming languages, coding software, and development tools I use are designed and maintained for free for users to learn. These people are often funded by donations. Other programs such as Java implement a free-to-users, expensive-for-businesses software model that enables anyone to learn and use the software while still providing funding for upkeep and maintenance for Java developers.

Edson reminds me of a new perspective, similar to the unnamed developers and programmers out there are all the artists, internet historians, and content creators. These people are contributors to the sum of human creativity and knowledge; they teach others and create spaces outside of capitalism. And yet, they won't receive the accolades they deserve. As Tim Oâ€™Reilly puts it capitalism measures "value captured, not value created."

Edson goes on to argue that our museums and intellectual institutions are behind the times on their digital prowess. They lack the openness that many artists and developers have tried to push for on the internet. And they fail to utilize digital media in ways that could enable more open access to art, culture, and knowledge. One way institutions can grow is by accepting help from the people who offer it. There are plenty out there who would gladly revamp websites and code apps for museums if just given the chance. Perhaps museums and intellectual institutions such as colleges would benefit from reaching out and partnering with others for technological help.

It's perhaps important to mention the ways that some institutions are using the internet to its fullest. Monterey Bay Aquarium, for example, offers live cameras of some of their exhibits online for anyone to view. They also run a Tumblr blog that interacts with the community, increasing awareness for events. Or some parks such as Central Park run Twitters that share information on their history and increase community engagement.

Of course, no amount of outside partnerships and asking for funding on GoFundMe and Patreon will ever replace adequately funding our cultural institutions. With the money that current institutions have it is still increasingly important, and beneficial, to invest in digital outreach. Now the largest audiences ever can be reached online. And while it may not be the same as going to a museum, there must be a way to interact with and enjoy institutional culture online.

Black boxes

9/16/22

Black Box

Ian Milligan's "History in the Age of Abundance?" examines a lot of interesting ideas in the first chapter. In this blog post I wanted to explore more about how the growing body of historical born digital data has to grapple with increasingly more "black boxes" of bias. In the digital era, many of our daily activities are automated behind complex algorithms without easily understandable methodologies. For example, a Google Search weighs in all of a user's previous searches, their location and advertising profile, as well as history and information from friends and family, when deciding which search results to return to a user. Our increasingly complex world has not moved on from the biases of history, technology has often only hidden them behind user-friendly disguises.

Another example of black boxes in the digital age is anything and everything to do with Machine Learning. Specifically neural networks. These algorithms use complex mathematics to learn to minimize an error function. With a enough data a neural network can learn to do whatever task it is assigned. Some AI's classify complex data into categories, such as a handwriting recognition device. Others are used to make decisions when given lots of interconnected data, such as which road to take given the current traffic, or which financial applicants qualify for a bank loan. In many of these examples bias is easily countered or identified, Google Maps uses an AI to determine your route but since route data isn't racist, it would be very hard to make a racist mapping AI. However, in the bank loan example, or in examples that involve making hiring and firing decisions, or AI's that choose how to curate information, racism is incredibly hard to avoid. Unless the data used to train an AI is perfectly unbiased, the AI will be biased as well. This is a terrible problem in the AI world as the interworkings of an artificial intelligence are purely mathematical, they are a "black box," without explanation or qualifiers to their answers.

Ian Milligan recognizes that's there's bias in every time period, every policy and every decision made. Our algorithms are necessarily going to be biased as well. Computer Scientists now are tasked with developing methods for reducing bias in AI's and almost as importantly, developing tools for explaining why an AI does what it does. Historians as well work towards eliminating bias in their work, by developing more inclusive policies, and more empathetic decision making. When developing tools for web scraping and archival we must approach the practice with the same intent. While we cannot remove bias altogether, scientists can make conscientious deciscions about which websites get archived and who's voice is heard.

In our increasingly interconnected world it becomes more important to recognize our own biases and the biases around us. As scientists we have the tools and resources to build a better and more inclusive world, and must strive to do so. Only by addressing our biases and developing tools and methodologies to counteract them can we document our world most accurately.

1962 Seattle World's Fair

10/11/22

Earth's first man-made satellite

Americans during the 1962 Seattle World's Fair were inundated with new ideas, inventions, and technology. The future seemed bright and the World's Fair was the place to be for exploring optimistic dreams about what the future might hold. The space race, for example, was a shining example of American ingenuity and prosperity. We were aiming for the stars and by 1962 we were on our way.

The Vanguard I model at the 1962 Seattle World's Fair was part of the Bell Labs exhibit showing the wonders of the future. The Vanguard I is the world's first man-made satellite and in 1962 was a state-of-the-art technological marvel. The satellite used brand new technologies, solar batteries and transistors, invented by Bell labs, and was approximately the size of a grapefruit. It was the 4th ever man-made object to successfully reach Earth orbit.

Importantly, Vanguard I was the first spacecraft that utilized solar batteries, a new invention by Bell labs. These batteries could be recharged via solar panel, allowing for the continued operation of the satellite in space. This new technology was part of a wave of rapidly developing space technologies that would inspire Americans to dream big about the future.

Vanguard I currently resides in Medium Earth Orbit, where it will stay for the next 240 some years before being dragged out of orbit by solar radiation and atmospheric drag. However, Vanguard I is not operational, its last radio signal was received in 1964, seven years after its launch. Since then Vanguard I has aptly served as a quiet vanguard over the Earth, a reminder of human ingenuity and progress. Vanguard I represents everything humanity reached for in 1962 and continues to serve as inspiration today.

Videogames

10/28/22

Skyrim player standing off with dragon, via GIPHY

Historical themed video games all have some level of fantasy as part of their structure. This fantasy tints our perspective of the past while playing these games. Some games, such as war games, give a false impression of history over time. There's more to civilization than our wars, but many games fail to represent life outside of combat. For example, games like Civilization can easily give false impressions about what it was like to live in the past. Society is not a strict linear progression of increasing technology and power. In the game however, players are constantly improving, building and expanding their territories. These historical innacuracies build up quickly, and importantly, an uneducated player has no way of distinguishing what is true to history and what's part of the game.

Video games such as Skyrim that offer a more in-depth fantasy world fall prey to the same issue. It offers a perspective on history that is less war focused, but is still largely combat and fighting based. I'd argue that Skyrim, as a first-person RPG game, can provide a unique perspective on history. The grime and the grit of living in the Middle Ages is palpable. The dim lighting is authentically irritaiting. Life for most non-player-characters seems rough. But at the same time, it makes being a theif look easy and fun. In Skyrim at least, the player is eventually and essentially all powerful. The more power that is given to the player, the more ability they have to deviate from history.

A player with no power follows a strict story and makes no choices. This is less of a game and more of a movie. A game where the player is all-powerful is similarly boring, without some sort of narrative limitations and struggle, there's no reason to play. A game that aims to teach about a historical period must find the sweetspot between empowering the player for a fun game and limiting the player to a realistic world.

One game, "This War of Mine," is an amazing and educational portrayal of not a historical period but of what it's like to live in a warzone. Game designers who want to build educational and meaningful games would do well to take inspiration from "This War of Mine." The architects of the game paid special attention to ensure that every game mechanic contributes to the overall goal of the narrative. Even on easy mode, life in a warzone is relentlessly difficult. One aspect that stands out for any historical narrative is the character bios. While playing, the characters that you control periodically update their biographies with their thoughts and feelings. Many of the most impactful moments in the game come from reading the very basic, human wants of your characters and then struggling to meet them from the challenge of living in a warzone.
